How a student put himself on the internet.

Building this site from a blank file — the choices, the lessons, the small joys of vanilla CSS.

By Toby Qian Published 11 May 2026 Guangzhou

The first version of this site was a single index.html on my desktop and one paragraph that started with hi, I'm Toby. That was at midnight. By 3 a.m. I had a top bar, three sections, and the conviction that I was a designer. By the next afternoon I had thrown the whole thing out and started over.

What follows are the small lessons that survived. None of them are new. All of them are mine.

One — own the file

I picked vanilla HTML and CSS for the same reason people still keep a physical notebook. The file in front of you is the file the browser sees. When something is wrong, the problem is on one screen.

Two — design with constraints, not options

Three fonts. Five colors. One layout grid. The page tightened up the moment I stopped picking new things and started reusing the ones I had.

Most websites would be better if their authors had to pay a small fee every time they introduced a new font weight.

Three — ship the awkward version

The first deploy felt premature. The mascot was wrong. The gallery had placeholder squares. I pushed it anyway, because the only way a personal site gets less awkward is by being out in the open.
